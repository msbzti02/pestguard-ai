COMPREHENSIVE PEST DATASHEET — COTTON/MELON APHID (Aphis gossypii)
====================================================================
Compiled from FAO, university extension services, and agricultural research databases.

TAXONOMY:
- Order: Hemiptera
- Family: Aphididae
- Scientific name: Aphis gossypii Glover
- Common names: Cotton aphid, Melon aphid, Cucurbit aphid

IDENTIFICATION:
- Body length: 1.0–1.8 mm (wingless), up to 2.2 mm (winged)
- Color: Variable — light green, dark green, yellowish, or nearly black
- Cornicles (tail pipes): Short, dark, cylindrical
- Antennae: Shorter than body length
- Distinguish from other aphids by smaller body size and darker cornicles

HOST PLANTS:
- Primary: Cotton, melon, cucumber, squash, watermelon
- Secondary: Citrus, eggplant, peppers, okra, hibiscus
- Over 700 host plant species recorded worldwide

GEOGRAPHIC DISTRIBUTION:
- Worldwide distribution in tropical, subtropical, and temperate regions
- Common in: Turkey, Mediterranean basin, South/Southeast Asia, Africa, Americas
- Active year-round in warm climates; seasonal in temperate zones

BIOLOGY AND LIFECYCLE:
- Reproduction: Parthenogenetic (asexual) in warm climates — females give live birth
- Generation time: 7–10 days at 25°C
- Offspring per female: 40–80 nymphs
- Population doubling time: As fast as 3–4 days under optimal conditions
- Overwinters as eggs on primary hosts in cold climates
- Winged forms develop when colonies become crowded (dispersal)

DAMAGE SYMPTOMS:
- Direct feeding: Sap sucking causes leaf curling, yellowing, and wilting
- Honeydew production: Sticky residue on leaves promotes sooty mold growth
- Virus transmission: Vector for over 50 plant viruses including:
  * Cucumber mosaic virus (CMV)
  * Cotton leaf curl virus
  * Watermelon mosaic virus
  * Zucchini yellow mosaic virus
- Yield impact: 20–50% yield reduction in severe infestations (cotton, cucurbits)

CHEMICAL CONTROL:
- Neonicotinoids:
  * Imidacloprid (Confidor/Gaucho): Systemic, effective but harmful to bees
  * Thiamethoxam (Actara): Foliar or soil drench application
  * Acetamiprid: Less toxic to bees than other neonicotinoids
- Selective aphicides:
  * Pymetrozine (Chess/Fulfill): Disrupts feeding behavior, safe for beneficials
  * Flonicamid (Beleaf): Systemic, low toxicity to natural enemies
- Pyrethroids:
  * Lambda-cyhalothrin: Broad-spectrum, use as last resort (kills beneficials)
- Insecticidal soaps: Potassium salts of fatty acids — safe for organic farming
- Neem oil: Azadirachtin-based — disrupts growth and feeding

APPLICATION TIMING:
- Scout fields 2x per week during critical growth stages
- Treat when: >50 aphids per leaf OR >25% of plants infested
- Apply early morning (before 10 AM) or late evening (after 5 PM)
- Do NOT spray during rain, wind >15 km/h, or temperature >35°C
- Pre-harvest interval: Check specific product label (typically 7–21 days)

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Predators:
  * Lady beetles (Coccinellidae): Adults eat 50+ aphids/day; larvae eat 200+ in lifetime
  * Green lacewings (Chrysoperla carnea): Larvae are voracious aphid feeders
  * Hoverfly larvae (Syrphidae): Each larva consumes 400+ aphids
  * Minute pirate bugs (Orius spp.): Effective in greenhouse and field
- Parasitoids:
  * Aphidius colemani: Lays eggs inside aphids; effective commercial biocontrol agent
  * Lysiphlebus testaceipes: Common natural parasitoid in cotton fields
- Microbial agents:
  * Beauveria bassiana: Entomopathogenic fungus — sprayed as biological insecticide
  * Lecanicillium lecanii: Effective under high humidity conditions

CULTURAL CONTROL:
- Remove and destroy heavily infested plant parts
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilization (promotes soft, attractive growth)
- Use reflective mulches (silver/aluminum) to repel winged aphids
- Intercropping with aromatic plants (basil, marigold) may reduce colonization
- Crop rotation with non-host plants
- Maintain field hygiene — remove volunteer plants and weeds

RESISTANCE MANAGEMENT:
- Aphis gossypii has developed resistance to multiple insecticide classes
- Known resistance: Organophosphates, carbamates, pyrethroids, neonicotinoids
- Rotate chemical classes with each application (Mode of Action rotation)
- Never apply same active ingredient more than 2 consecutive times
- Use IPM approach to reduce selection pressure

DISCLAIMER: This information is for guidance only. Consult a certified agricultural expert before application. Always follow product label instructions.


COMPREHENSIVE PEST DATASHEET — ASIATIC RICE STEM BORER (Chilo suppressalis)
=============================================================================

TAXONOMY:
- Order: Lepidoptera
- Family: Crambidae
- Scientific name: Chilo suppressalis Walker
- Common names: Asiatic rice borer, Striped rice stem borer, Striped rice borer

IDENTIFICATION:
- Adult moth: Wingspan 20–30 mm, straw-colored with dark central streak on forewing
- Larvae: Cream to yellowish-white, up to 25 mm, with 5 brown longitudinal stripes
- Pupae: Brown, found inside rice stems

HOST PLANTS:
- Primary: Rice (Oryza sativa)
- Secondary: Maize, sugarcane, millet, wild grasses (Poaceae family)

GEOGRAPHIC DISTRIBUTION:
- Native to: East and Southeast Asia
- Invasive in: Southern Europe (Spain, Italy, Portugal), North Africa
- Major pest in: China, Japan, Korea, India, Bangladesh, Vietnam, Turkey

BIOLOGY AND LIFECYCLE:
- Generations per year: 2–4 depending on climate
- Eggs: Laid in overlapping rows on leaf surface (50–100 eggs per mass)
- Egg incubation: 5–10 days
- Larval stages: 5–6 instars over 25–40 days
- Pupation: Inside stem, lasting 7–12 days
- Adult lifespan: 5–10 days
- Overwinters as mature larva inside rice stubble

DAMAGE SYMPTOMS:
- Deadheart: Young larvae bore into tillers, killing the growing point → central shoot turns brown
- Whitehead: Later infestation causes empty panicles that turn white at heading stage
- Stem tunneling: Internal feeding weakens stems, causing lodging (plant falls over)
- Economic threshold: Treat when >10% tillers show deadheart OR >5% whiteheads
- Yield loss: 10–40% depending on infestation timing and severity

CHEMICAL CONTROL:
- Granular insecticides (applied to paddy water):
  * Carbofuran 3G: Broadcast at 33 kg/ha into standing water
  * Fipronil 0.3G: Effective but restricted in some countries due to environmental concerns
- Foliar sprays:
  * Chlorantraniliprole (Coragen): Low toxicity, long residual activity
  * Flubendiamide: Effective against lepidopteran borers
  * Cartap hydrochloride: Systemic activity
  * Triazophos: Broad-spectrum (less preferred due to toxicity)
- Application timing:
  * First application: At egg hatching (7–10 days after moth flight peak)
  * Granules: Apply before larvae bore into stems
  * Critical window: Missing this window renders chemical control ineffective

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Egg parasitoids:
  * Trichogramma japonicum: Release 100,000–150,000 per hectare at egg-laying period
  * Telenomus rowani: Natural parasitoid, conserve by reducing broad-spectrum sprays
- Larval parasitoids:
  * Cotesia flavipes: Braconid wasp, attacks young larvae
- Predators:
  * Spiders (Lycosa, Tetragnatha): Major predators in rice paddies
  * Dragonflies: Feed on adult moths
  * Frogs: Consume larvae and pupae
- Microbial control:
  * Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt): Spray formulation effective on young larvae

CULTURAL CONTROL:
- Harvest at ground level (cut stubble short) to destroy overwintering larvae
- Plow and submerge stubble after harvest to kill pupae
- Synchronize planting dates within a region (reduces continuous food supply)
- Use light traps to monitor and reduce adult moth populations
- Remove and destroy egg masses by clipping leaf tips during transplanting
- Avoid excessive nitrogen (promotes lush growth attractive to borers)

INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT (IPM) STRATEGY:
1. Monitor: Use light traps and field scouting weekly
2. Threshold: Act only when damage exceeds economic threshold (10% deadhearts)
3. Biological first: Release Trichogramma at egg-laying period
4. Chemical last: Use selective insecticides only when biocontrol is insufficient
5. Post-harvest: Destroy stubble to break pest cycle

DISCLAIMER: This information is for guidance only. Consult a certified agricultural expert before application.


COMPREHENSIVE PEST DATASHEET — EUROPEAN CORN BORER (Ostrinia nubilalis)
========================================================================

TAXONOMY:
- Order: Lepidoptera
- Family: Crambidae
- Scientific name: Ostrinia nubilalis Hübner
- Common names: European corn borer, ECB

IDENTIFICATION:
- Adult moth: Wingspan 25–35 mm; males darker than females
- Female: Pale yellow-brown with darker zigzag bands across wings
- Male: Darker brown with olive tinge
- Larvae: Flesh-colored with dark spots along body, dark head capsule, up to 25 mm
- Eggs: Flat, overlapping clusters on underside of leaves (15–30 eggs)

HOST PLANTS:
- Primary: Corn/maize (Zea mays)
- Secondary: Peppers, beans, potatoes, cotton, sorghum, millet
- Also attacks: Hemp, hops, rhubarb, and many broadleaf weeds

DAMAGE AND ECONOMIC IMPACT:
- Annual losses: Estimated $1 billion+ in the US alone before Bt corn adoption
- Stalk boring: Larvae tunnel through stalks weakening plant structure
- Ear damage: Larvae bore into ears, creating entry points for fungal diseases
- Yield loss: 5–30% depending on infestation severity
- Quality loss: Fungal contamination (mycotoxins) in damaged ears

CHEMICAL CONTROL:
- Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis):
  * Bt var. kurstaki: Spray at early larval stages (before stem boring)
  * Must contact larvae — ineffective after they enter the stalk
- Synthetic insecticides:
  * Chlorantraniliprole: Excellent residual activity
  * Bifenthrin: Pyrethroid, broad-spectrum
  * Spinosad: Derived from soil bacterium, reduced risk to beneficials
- Timing is critical: Spray within 7 days of egg hatch, before larvae bore into stalk

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Trichogramma ostriniae: Egg parasitoid — release 200,000 per hectare at egg-laying
- Macrocentrus grandii: Larval parasitoid
- Lady beetles: Feed on egg masses
- Bt corn varieties: Transgenic corn expressing Bt toxin (most effective solution)

CULTURAL CONTROL:
- Destroy crop residues after harvest (shred and plow under stalks)
- Rotate crops — corn after corn increases borer pressure
- Early planting reduces exposure to second-generation borers
- Maintain proper plant nutrition (not excessive nitrogen)

DISCLAIMER: This information is for guidance only. Consult a certified agricultural expert before application.


COMPREHENSIVE PEST DATASHEET — FALL ARMYWORM (Spodoptera frugiperda)
=====================================================================

TAXONOMY:
- Order: Lepidoptera
- Family: Noctuidae
- Scientific name: Spodoptera frugiperda J.E. Smith
- Common names: Fall armyworm, FAW

IDENTIFICATION:
- Larvae: Green to brown with dark stripes; inverted Y-shape on head capsule (key feature)
- Adults: Gray-brown moth, wingspan 32–40 mm; males have distinctive wing markings
- Eggs: Dome-shaped clusters covered with scales (100–200 eggs)

DAMAGE:
- Attacks: Maize, rice, sorghum, sugarcane, wheat, vegetables
- Larvae feed on leaves creating ragged holes and windowpane damage
- In corn: Burrow into whorl, destroying growing point
- Yield loss: 20–73% in maize (Africa/Asia since 2016 invasion)

CHEMICAL CONTROL:
- Emamectin benzoate: Most effective synthetic option
- Spinetoram / Spinosad: Reduced-risk, effective on young larvae
- Chlorantraniliprole: Systemic, long-lasting
- Avoid pyrethroids: FAW has widespread resistance
- Spray in evening (larvae feed at night)

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Telenomus remus: Egg parasitoid — most promising biocontrol agent
- Trichogramma spp.: Egg parasitoid
- NPV (Nuclear Polyhedrosis Virus): Spodoptera-specific baculovirus
- Cotesia icipe: Larval parasitoid (discovered in Kenya)

CULTURAL CONTROL:
- Push-pull technology: Intercrop with Desmodium (repels) and Napier grass (attracts/traps)
- Early planting to avoid peak moth flights
- Handpicking larvae from whorl (small-scale farmers)
- Apply sand + lime into whorl to suffocate larvae

DISCLAIMER: This information is for guidance only. Consult a certified agricultural expert before application.


COMPREHENSIVE PEST DATASHEET — CITRUS LEAF MINER (Phyllocnistis citrella)
==========================================================================

TAXONOMY:
- Order: Lepidoptera
- Family: Gracillariidae
- Scientific name: Phyllocnistis citrella Stainton
- Common names: Citrus leafminer, CLM

IDENTIFICATION:
- Adult: Tiny moth (wingspan 4–5 mm), silvery white with dark wing tip spot
- Larvae: Pale green to transparent, found inside serpentine leaf mines
- Pupae: In rolled leaf margin
- Mines: Silver serpentine trails visible on leaf surface (diagnostic feature)

DAMAGE:
- Larvae mine between leaf surfaces creating winding tunnels
- Young flush growth is most susceptible
- Severe: Leaf curling, distortion, reduced photosynthesis
- Creates wounds that facilitate citrus canker infection
- Yield impact: Primarily affects tree vigor rather than direct fruit loss
- Most damaging to nursery trees and young orchards

CHEMICAL CONTROL:
- Abamectin: Effective foliar spray
- Imidacloprid: Soil drench for systemic protection of new flush
- Spinosad: Reduced risk, effective when applied to new growth
- Neem oil: Deterrent and growth regulator
- Mineral oil sprays: Suffocate larvae in mines

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Ageniaspis citricola: Introduced parasitoid — highly effective (up to 90% parasitism)
- Cirrospilus spp.: Native parasitoid wasps
- Semielacher petiolatus: Ectoparasitoid of larvae
- Conserve natural enemies by minimizing broad-spectrum sprays

CULTURAL CONTROL:
- Prune to manage flush timing (synchronize growth to reduce mining period)
- Remove water sprouts (sucker growth) which are highly susceptible
- Avoid excessive nitrogen that promotes continuous flush growth
- In nurseries: Use protective netting over young trees

DISCLAIMER: This information is for guidance only. Consult a certified agricultural expert before application.


COMPREHENSIVE PEST DATASHEET — MIGRATORY LOCUST (Locusta migratoria)
======================================================================

TAXONOMY:
- Order: Orthoptera
- Family: Acrididae
- Scientific name: Locusta migratoria Linnaeus
- Common names: Migratory locust, African migratory locust

IDENTIFICATION:
- Solitary phase: Green or brown, 40–55 mm body length
- Gregarious phase: Yellow-brown with dark markings, longer wings
- Nymphs (hoppers): 5 instars, change color with phase
- Key feature: Phase polyphenism — changes behavior, color, and shape based on density

DAMAGE:
- Polyphagous: Eats virtually all green vegetation
- Swarms can contain billions of individuals
- A small swarm (1 km²) can consume food equivalent to 35,000 people/day
- Complete crop destruction possible in hours
- Affects: Cereals, pastures, vegetables, fruit trees, forests

CHEMICAL CONTROL:
- Ultra-low volume (ULV) spraying:
  * Fenitrothion: Most commonly used organophosphate
  * Malathion: Alternative organophosphate
  * Deltamethrin: Pyrethroid, effective barrier treatment
  * Lambda-cyhalothrin: Contact and residual action
- Application method: Aerial spraying for swarms, ground spraying for hopper bands
- Timing: Most effective against hopper bands before fledging

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Metarhizium acridum (Green Muscle): Fungal biopesticide specific to locusts
- Advantages: No harm to non-target organisms, persists in environment
- Disadvantages: Slower kill (7–14 days) compared to chemicals
- Nosema locustae: Microsporidian pathogen

MONITORING AND EARLY WARNING:
- Regular field surveys of known breeding areas
- Satellite imagery for vegetation monitoring (identifies breeding conditions)
- Report sightings to national locust control center
- Phase change monitoring: Crowding triggers gregarious behavior

DISCLAIMER: This information is for guidance only. Consult a certified agricultural expert before application.
